Victims in special periods:

Hong Kong journalists are being hurt by gender

Since June, successive waves of protests against a controversial extradition bill have occurred every weekend in Hong Kong. Over time, the conflict associated with the demonstrations has intensified, involving journalists to implicate certain disturbances.

Being blamed as “搏出位” (drawing attention) and a “妓者” (a word combining prostitute and journalist), Amy Ip, a freelance female journalist, said in an interview with Stand News that her name and photo are also spread in online groups of pro-establishment camp. These all happened after she read out a statement in a press conference criticizing the police force treated media workers violently.

On 28 October, Amy protested at the police press conference by reading out the statement and pointing a flashlight at the police officials to demonstrate how police treated journalists during the protest.

Verbal attacks targeting the gender of journalists specifically happen on females, including wordings about journalists’ appearance and sex. Wordings like “妓者” (a word combining prostitute and journalist), “臭雞” (smelly chicken, while the chicken is often used to describe prostitute) and “八婆” (gossip old lady, means similar to bitch) is commonly used to describe females rudely, including female journalists.

In the recent ongoing protests across the city, journalists face attacks, including verbal and physical, during their reporting work. Journalists of different genders may face different levels of threats. Jessica Lo, a part-time year-4 journalist in nowTV, said that female journalists face more personal attacks because of gender and appearance.

Jessica Lo: Verbal abuse against my gender hurts me

Jessica Lo, a year 4 student, studying journalism and mass communication in Hong Kong Shue Yan University. She had an internship in NowTV from June to August and now is having part-time there. She is responsible for Hong Kong internal issue for the past few months. Interviewing the rally organized by Politihk Social Strategic outside RTHK on 24th August is one of her tasks. She thought it would be normal as interviewing other protests.



Q: Can you briefly describe the situation of that day?

J: I arrived outside of RTHK alone at around 2 pm. My cameraman was still on the way from the office, so I decided to use my mobile phone to take some footage. Before taking the videos, I wore a yellow vest and put on press card to avoid any misunderstanding from the crowds. However, some participants surrounded me and stopped me from videotaping.

Q: Have you encountered any problems during the protest, such as physically and verbally?

J: I was kicked two times by a stranger and a woman during walking beside RTHK. When I was filming while taking videos, someone hit my camera and nearly fell to the ground. For verbally, some participants pointed at me and almost touched my eyes. As most of the participants are male and most of the journalists are female, nearly all female journalists are blamed and encircled. They said some words that were targeting at the female.

Q: Do you think it is sexual harassment?

J: Yes, I will regard this as sexual harassment. They said tones of sexual words. Though I did not face any sexual harassment physically before, I know some girls have experienced that.

Q: What do you feel when you hear those words?

J: I admit I feel scared, but I know it was useless to argue with them. I do feel frustrated because of his action at that moment, but I can hardly hear what he said. When I go back home and watch the playback, I can clearly understand what he said. (Taking a deep breath)I must commit that I have hard feelings after watching those videos. It is hard to stay calm with all the insult.

Q: How can you cope with hard feelings? Did you cry?

J: No, I did not cry. But I feel upset. I just think that doing an interview is my job and I want to report the truth. Why do they need to treat me like that? Maybe I’m still fresh. I know some journalists might get used to it, but I really cannot stand and remain silent.

Q: Do you think male journalists faceless sexual abuse than female journalists?

J: Yes. I think male journalists will face less sexual insult as those words do not work for them. They are usually attacked because of their professions. I believe that female journalists face more personal attacks because of gender and appearance.

Q: Did you report to NowTV immediately? What did they do to help you?

J: Yes, NowTV sent another crew to help us later. Moreover, NowTV reported to the police at once, but you know it doesn’t help much at all. I expect no people will be arrested in this event.

Q: Do you think that the whole of society has gender discrimination for the journalist industry?

J: Kind of. The image of male journalists is smart and familiar with numbers. Thus, they are usually responsible for financial news. However, there are also advantages of female journalists. For example, we are always regarded as caring and talkative. Investigative reporting and feature stories are usually packed by us.

Q: What do you think your agency can help? Is there any pre- or pro-protest assistance for the journalists?

J: I think the media agency can provide mental support for full-time journalists on how to get rid of unhappiness. They provide training to all journalists for preparation, but it is mainly focused on practical skills.


In one recent survey by Hong Kong Baptist University, 37.5% of the reporters said that the verbal abuse came from the police, 22.73% from the normal citizens, 20.45% from the protesters , 6.82% from the person in the same occupation and 1.14% from the Fujianese living in North Point . Among them, 51.22% of the reporters think the abuse is targeted at the gender of journalists clearly as well 55% said that their work mood was affected.




Vimvam Tong: The police see me as an incompetent little girl

Vimvam Tong, as intern journalism for Reuters, she spent two and a half months (June to mid-August) on the front line, participated in the coverage of most of the demonstrations during this period. Tong said she was "liberated," who was able to leave such a high-intensity environment early and went to Denmark as an exchange student.

Vimvam Tong waited for interview at the demostration site

By: Reuters

Q：After this period, can you recall one most memorable interview experience?

V: That must be June 30, one Sunday. I participated in the reporting both the Pro-Democratic Parade near Legislative Council and pro-police rally in the Tamar Park not far away. The interviews on two sides left me with a deep impression, especially in the pro-police rally.

Most of the supporters are middle-aged, who are very sensitive to my identity as a journalist. They will charge us for news reporting that the story is biased and challenging the professionalism of journalists, particularly young female journalists. They blame us for being the mouthpiece of the mob and beautifying the behavior of the demonstrators opposed to police, and this is not the case.

Q: In addition to questioning the professionalism of journalists, is there any discrimination against the gender of journalists?

V: I didn't encounter some insults targeting women at that time (June 30) explicitly. However, in the later interview of "Reclaim Sheung Shui," we were subjected to some unwarranted scolding and shirking from the police. Still remember some other female journalists have indicated that the police have physically contacted them, warning the behavior of the police on the spot is sexual harassment.

In fact, female reporters will be more easily despised and ordered, and the police will be able to ask female reporters to avoid some reporting. Simultaneously, my male colleague will not be as much attention as the police. The fun thing is that I often receive water from the police and the gas mask handed to me by the demonstrators during the protest. It may be that girls need to gain more attention on the front-line in most ordinary people's minds.

Q: As you said, your male colleague will not be as much attention as the police, can you elaborate it specifically?

V: Well, as an intern reporter, I don't really have much experience in on-site reporting. During a demonstration near Hong Kong Liaison Office, the police have raised their warning flag to indicate that they will release tear gas. I didn't realize to forget to bring equipment such as gas masks until then so that I turned to the police for help. Nevertheless, they just want to drive me away and leave the scene to run in a safe place. Their attitude is like saying that a little girl does not need to appear on the stage.

Similar mistrust can also happen in my news agency. As far as my editor is concerned, she will have more consideration for female reporters. In her cognition, men usually gain more strength in communication with police or demonstrators. When assigning interviews, she will definitely send a full-time male reporter, and there will be some special precautions before the interview.

It's hard to judge whether it's sex discrimination, but it is always considered that it is impossible to allow me to be on the front line full of gunpowder.

Q: How does this nearly three-month experience affect your mood?

V: No doubt, there will be some trauma and cracks in my inner center. For example, every time I see hundreds of heavy-duty explosion-proof police officers knocking on the shield loudly, shouting "go away" with a significant decibel continuously, as if to knock us back. But as a journalist, I should stick to the front line.

The mental shadow of the verbal attack is also somber. If you participate in the pro-police rally, you will not wear clad in black, try to wear light-colored clothes, even no mask.

Q: Are these traumata repairable? Do you think your news agency needs to provide corresponding help?

V: Whether in the past few months, Hong Kong's political atmosphere or its own working environment is very repressed. There's always distrust between the two parties(police and demonstrators). If there is a slightly biased content reported by the journalists, the buzz of the other party will appear.

I think the best way is solving by yourself. It is also allowed if you want to seek some counseling from the news agency. At the same time, my editor-in-chief also talks to me from time to time and cares about my physical and mental health. If you feel uncomfortable, you are also very free to allow leave.


There are 41.07% of journalists think the editor will consider their gender when sending them to the first-line interview demonstrations. 13.79% believe there’s gender discrimination in the news agency while 23.73% believe there’s gender discrimination against women in the entire Hong Kong society for journalists.





Mental needs and support for journalists

Journalists on the frontline understand they may be hurt anytime which is also a kind of pressure we are suffering.
Bethanie Ho

“We become more sensitive after covering the protests in this period, especially when we smell the smell of tear gas or hear a sudden loud voice in normal days,” said Bethanie Ho, a journalist, working in Ming Pao, who graduated from Hong Kong Baptist University 3 years ago.

Covering the protests put different mental pressure on journalists. And they suffer from mixed emotions after covering the protests.

On 12th June, it is the start of the movement’s forced upgrade. “After that day, we need to go back to finish our news story with a calm and professional attitude under the chaotic situation. I feel difficult to pull myself away from the emotion that night after I finished my story.”

“Journalists on the frontline understand they may be hurt anytime which is also a kind of pressure we are suffering,” Ho added. “For myself, I feel the worst on 17th June. I saw the changes in the movement and felt powerless.” On 17th June, a man committed suicide with his political appeal by jumping down from Pacific Place in Admiralty.

In the survey, 89.09% of the reporters agree that news agencies should provide journalists with emotional and psychological counseling.


When asked whether journalists need psychological counseling, Ho and her colleague Alpha Chan show their support for it.

“Journalists’ mental health is something that should be focused on. Especially when journalists always witness things that normal people don’t experience,” said Alpha Chan, also a journalist working in Ming Pao. She mentioned that journalists might not feel well after reporting on different accidents, especially severe accidents like Amoycan Industrial Centre fire in 2016, double-decker bus flipped accident in Tai Po in 2018, and also the protest ongoing now.

Their news agency arranged talks and sharing, like inviting psychological professors to support journalists psychologically. These kinds of support are not provided to every journalist. Jessica Lo suggested that the agency can give mental assistance to all journalists, especially full-time and front-line staff.

“If there is mental assistance provided, I would leave the chance to other journalists as I didn’t suffer from serious trauma after reporting on the protests. I think the more front-line journalists may need it more,” said Anna Kam, a year 4 student majoring in International Journalism at Hong Kong Baptist University. She worked as a freelance journalist for four international media agencies. There is no specific mental support or counseling provided.

Can law protection protect female journalists?

From the survey, 44.59% of the reporters said that the physical conflict came from the police, 17.57% from the demonstrators, 12.16% from the normal citizens, and 8.11% from the person in the same occupation. Among them, 15.79% of the reporters think the insult is targeted at the gender of journalists clearly as well 56.76% said that their work mood was affected by physical conflicts.


When reporters encounter some physical abuse during reporting, such as sexual harassment, perhaps there are already relevant laws that can protect them.

As one of the discrimination law ordinances in Hong Kong, Sex Discrimination Ordinance (SDO) protects a person from sexual harassment and victimization in prescribed areas of activities, including employment.

“Journalists and practitioners in the media industry have the same protection from sexual harassment and sex discrimination under the SDO.,” claimed Dorothy Sam, Corporate Communications Manager of Equal Opportunity Commission (EOC) in a written reply.

Equal Opportunity Commission (EOC) handles complaints from people who feel that they have been discriminated against on any of the protected grounds. In Sam’s reply, the EOC endeavors to resolve the cases, which have been considered appropriate after examines all information relevant through conciliation, helping the disputing parties reach a settlement including assists with legal advice, legal assistance, and other forms of support.

Sam pointed out that the complaint figures are not categorized into different industries, so there are no actual figures for complaints from journalists at this moment. But in this period, when covering the protests, it may be a doubt whether journalists are free to send complaints to EOC to seek help.

TA journalist who doesn’t want to show her name pointed out the EOC seldom follow-up on any investigations it did. It didn’t respond to the gender issues faced by journalists actively until it is urged. The gender issues female journalists faced are not started since these protests but at an earlier time or maybe common practice.

The social status of journalists is improving, but news agency needs to do more

"From the disparity in gender wage income to the participation of labor within the labor market, there are still no equal job opportunities for both of the genders in nowadays Hong Kong society," said Social Studies Professor Choi Susanne Yuk-ping, the CUHK gender experts. "Sexual harassment has emerged across the industries, mostly based on gender bias and power inequality."

Choi added that, the dilemma faced by journalists might be more complicated because it involves not only sexual harassment within the news agency but in the face of interviewees. It's quite challenging for a journalist to complain or report at the scene because they have limited abuser's information if there is someone molesting.

In fact, Hong Kong has laws on sexual harassment - Sex Discrimination Ordinance and the agency dealing with sexual harassment - Equal Opportunities Commission, but these mechanisms still have shortcomings.

“If sexual harassment is civil conflict-related, such as verbal harassment, the only way to complain is to go to the EOC. The question is, attacks from interviewees are difficult to characterize unless they can capture the exact perpetrator on the spot. Besides, the current ordinance does not cover sexual harassment from interviewees necessarily.” said Choi, “the laws do regulate the employer that if the employee is sexually harassed, the employer will also be subject to corresponding criminal responsibility. ”

Choi believes that the social status of Hong Kong journalists is significantly improving during and after this political disturbance, but news agencies can provide more specialized training to journalists, at least to let them know if they have to deal with the situation. At the same time, the corresponding intra-company psychological counseling is also very significant.

"Many news agency actually fail to set special mechanisms to handle such questions because the ordinance does not stipulate that companies must do so."

Gender Issues not only happened when covering protest, but also in whole industry

You are not allowed to do that assignment. Not because of your experience, but because you are born to be a female.”
Experienced journalists

"You are not allowed to do that assignment. Not because of your experience, but because you are born to be a female.” Such voice is not uncommon to be heard in a newsroom.

A female journalist, who does not want to disclose her name, tells about how female journalists work in her former workplace, a TV news station.

Female journalists are not allowed to interview “dangerous” situations. For example, in typhoon, female journalists are not allowed to go out as they look weaker. This practice starts after the TV station received complaints about being harsh to female journalists by sending them out under extreme weather. Some people may regard this as a kind of protection for females. However, the ultimate goal for a journalist is to reveal the truth and disclose the secret to the public. Female journalists may also want to have the chance to report on different kinds of news. These are not merely safety concern, but a counter-discrimination towards female journalists.

"Gender dominant happens in the newsroom and outside the newsroom," she said. Neglect the fact that the staff of the management level is usually male, and gender issues also happen when journalists are reporting.

A journalist, usually a female in Hong Kong, go out do reporting work with a cameraman, usually a male. They cooperate in deciding how they report on the news. Think about a case, will an experienced male cameraman listen to the idea of a young less experienced female journalist? This combination is common in reality.

The number of female dominant in the industry, but the power seems not to come together.

Julie Posetti, who is an Australian journalist, studies cyberharassment and discovers the attacks against women journalists. It is mainly targeting on physical appearance and sexual orientation. She mentioned women reporters were called words like 'slut,' 'bitch,' or 'presstitute.'

According to a research constructed by a non-profit organization in Britain in 2014, female journalists and TV news presenters receiving roughly three times as much abuse as their male counterparts.
